First Post

2025.04.11 - English

This is the first post in my blog. I will probably be writing about random things of interest here.

As I have not much to talk about, in this first post I will just be sharing a very bad method of calculating square roots I came up with many years ago.

First, we section off the square root function every square number then linearly interpolate. Say we want to find the square root of $x$. Let us first call $a^2$ the largest perfect square smaller than $x$ and $b^2$ the smallest perfect square larger than $x$. Then, $\sqrt{x}$ can be found via linear interpolation:

$$ \sqrt{x} \approx a + \frac{x - a^2}{b^2 - a^2} $$

To improve this first approximation, which we will call $y_n$, we calculate the error $e$ of our approximation:

$$ e = x - y_n^2 $$

Then we can do some algebra to obtain:

$$ \begin{align} x - y_n^2 &= \left(\sqrt{x} + y_1\right)\left(\sqrt{x} - y_n\right) = e \\ \therefore \sqrt{x} &= \frac{e}{\sqrt{x} + y_n} + y_n \end{align} $$

If we approximate that $y_n$ is close enough to $\sqrt{x}$, we can write that the next approximation $y_{n+1}$ can be obtained by:

$$ y_{n+1} = \frac{e}{2y_n} + y_n $$

The above formula can be repeated as many times as needed. This method however is very subpar as there is a need to calculate the error every step.

I will give an example with $x = 44$. We find that:

$$ \begin{alignat}{4} a^2 &= 36 & \enspace\enspace\enspace a &= 6 \\ b^2 &= 59 & b &= 7 \\ \end{alignat} $$ Therefore $y_1$ is: $$ y_1 = 6 + \frac{44 - 36}{49 - 36} = \frac{86}{13} = 6.61538 $$ The error $e$ of this approximation is: $$ e = 44 - \left(\frac{86}{13}\right)^2 = 0.23669 $$ So the second approximation is given as: $$ y_2 = \frac{0.23669}{2 \cdot 86/13} + 86/13 = 6.63327 $$ The true square root of $44$ is: $$ \sqrt{44} = 6.63324 $$
